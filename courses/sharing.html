Sharing is more convenient!

One of the most convenient features of Notion is its permission Sharing, allowing you to share permissions on any page or layer, and its UX is very easy to understand.

  • Author
  • STAR Chang

Permission Settings

One of my favorite features of Notion is its collaboration capabilities. It is very easy to set permissions, and the recipients of these permissions can be team members, Notion users, or guests. This is extremely helpful for digital industries like ours that often require collaboration with clients or external partners.

Clicking on the "Share" option at the top allows you to split it into two sections: "Invite collaborators to edit" and "Share the page with visitors".

The URLs also differ. Directly copying the URL displayed in the browser is for sharing with team members, while the URL provided in the Share menu is for sharing with visitors.

Inviting Collaborators to Edit

Your collaborators need to first register for a free Notion account and log in. Then you can invite them by adding their email to the management. You can set the following different permissions for each collaborator:

  • Full Access: This means they have full permissions, including the ability to invite others to manage the page.
  • Can Edit: This means they can only edit the page, leave comments, and view.
  • Can Comment: This means they can only leave comments and view.
  • Can View: This means they can only view the page.
  • No Access: This means they have no access permissions at all.

Sharing Pages with Visitors

When sharing pages, there are several permission settings available, but they cannot be set individually for each person. Instead, they apply to all visitors.

  • Link expires: This allows you to set an expiration period for the shared page. After the specified period, the page will revert to being private.
  • Allow editing: This allows visitors who are logged into Notion but are not part of the management team to edit the page.
  • Allow comments: This allows visitors who are logged into Notion but are not part of the management team to leave comments.
  • Allow duplicate as template: This allows visitors who are logged into Notion but are not part of the management team to make a copy of the page into their own Notion workspace.

If visitors are not logged into Notion, they can only view the page.

Architecture of Sharing

The architecture of sharing is the essence of Notion's permission system, allowing you to freely configure the levels of access you want to share. The "Architecture of Sharing" applies to both "Inviting collaborators to edit" and "Sharing pages with visitors".

Notion's permission architecture is hierarchical and compatible. Having higher-level access also means having access to lower-level permissions. For example, if you share management access to the Homepage with your collaborators, it means they will also have management access to the underlying Projects and Finance sections. You have the flexibility to block specific lower-level permissions or selectively grant access to certain lower-level areas.

The following example illustrates different scenarios within a 4-level architecture.

Blocking the 2nd Layer

As the owner of the Homepage, you initially have access to all the pages within the Homepage, including Projects and Finance. However, if the 2nd layer, specifically Finance, is blocked, it means that none of the pages within the Finance section can be accessed.

Blocking the 2nd Layer and Opening the 3rd Layer

Continuing from the previous example, where the 2nd layer Finance is blocked, but the Clients section is relevant to you. Therefore, in this case, the management permission for the Clients section is opened to you from the 3rd layer.

Opening Permission for Only One Page in the Lowest Layer

In this scenario, only the permission for a specific page in the 4th layer is granted, while the permission for other pages is not opened.
